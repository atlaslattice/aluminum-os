## Council-Wide Terms of Service Conflict Audit

### Executive Summary

All ten Council-affiliated AI providers impose some form of **non-compete or competitive-use restriction** in their Terms of Service. These clauses, read broadly, could be interpreted to prohibit the very multi-model orchestration that Aluminum OS is designed to perform. Analysis across 40+ primary and secondary sources reveals a structural tension: the architecture's constitutional governance layer is designed to be vendor‑neutral, but every vendor's TOS attempts to constrain how its models may be used in relation to competing models.

The paper *"The Mirage of Artificial Intelligence Terms of Use Restrictions"* (Henderson et al., Indiana Law Journal, 2024) systematically assesses the legal enforceability of these clauses and concludes they are "largely unenforceable" due to lack of copyright in model weights/outputs, copyright preemption of state-law claims, and anti‑competitive overbreadth. This does not eliminate the risk, but it means the legal threat is primarily a chilling effect rather than a reliably enforceable claim.

Below is a seat‑by‑seat analysis, conflict severity assessment, and the existing Aluminum OS mitigations that apply.


### §1. Seat‑by‑Seat TOS Conflict Analysis

#### S1 – Anthropic / Claude

| Clause | Source | Text (paraphrased) |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| Commercial Non‑Compete | Commercial Terms §D.4 | Customer "may not and must not attempt to…access the Services to build a competing product or service, including to train competing AI models or resell the Services except as expressly approved by Anthropic." |
| Consumer Non‑Compete | Consumer Terms §3 | Prohibits using Claude "to develop any products or services that compete with our Services, including to develop or train any artificial intelligence or machine learning algorithms or models." |
| Third‑Party Harness Ban | Consumer Terms §3.7 | Subscribers may not use OAuth tokens "in any other product, tool, or service" – effectively forbidding third‑party orchestration harnesses. |
| Capacity Restrictions | Policy Change (April 2026) | Starting April 4, 2026, Claude subscriptions no longer work with third‑party agentic tools like OpenClaw; API or pay‑as‑you‑go billing required. |

**Conflict Severity: CRITICAL.** Anthropic's D.4 clause is the most precisely worded non‑compete in the industry. A multi‑model router that directs queries to Anthropic while also directing to competing models could be interpreted as "building a competing product." The third‑party harness ban explicitly targets the kind of orchestration the Switzerland Layer provides.

**Mitigations in Architecture:** Anthropic's API (as opposed to consumer subscriptions) is not subject to the harness ban. Aluminum OS routes through API keys, not OAuth tokens. The Council seat architecture with COI disclosure (D‑25) and stacked‑incentive transparency (D‑84) means Anthropic's participation is acknowledged as self‑interested. The architecture includes explicit Anthropic substitution pathways (§8.8 of OS spec).

### §2. Cross‑Cutting Patterns

**Pattern 1: The Non‑Compete Stack.** All five Western frontier labs (Anthropic, Google, Microsoft, OpenAI, xAI) impose some form of "do not use our service to compete with us" clause. Three (Anthropic, xAI, Notion) extend this to "competing services" broadly, not just competing models. Read literally, a multi‑model orchestration layer that routes among providers could be deemed a competing service by any of them.

**Pattern 2: The Third‑Party Harness Crackdown.** Anthropic (OpenClaw ban), Google (Antigravity suspensions), and GitHub (usage caps) are all actively tightening restrictions on third‑party tools that route prompts across models. Aluminum OS's Switzerland Layer is architecturally a third‑party harness from each provider's perspective.

**Pattern 3: Chinese Regulatory Asymmetry.** DeepSeek and Qwen's TOS focus on Chinese law compliance (content filtering, real‑name verification, export controls) rather than competitive prohibition. This creates an asymmetric risk profile: Western providers can restrict on competitive grounds; Chinese providers restrict on regulatory grounds.

**Pattern 4: The Enforceability Question.** The Henderson et al. (2024) paper in the *Indiana Law Journal* systematically argues these non‑compete clauses are largely unenforceable because model weights and outputs are not copyrightable, copyright preemption doctrines limit state‑law claims, and anti‑competitive provisions fare worse than responsible‑use provisions in court. No major AI provider has actually attempted to enforce these clauses through monetary penalties or injunctive relief.

### §5. Recommended New Mitigations

Based on this analysis, three new measures are recommended:

1. **Proposed Doctrine 92 – TOS Override Protocol:** When a provider's TOS restricts inter‑model routing, the architecture shall default to sovereign‑pathway deployment (air‑gapped, local model, or enterprise API) for that provider's models. No provider shall be excluded from the routing table solely due to an untested TOS clause, but every routing decision must carry a `tos_compliance_status` field in the TransparencyPacket.

2. **Proposed Risk Vector R66 – TOS Enforcement Event:** Track the probability of any Council provider actually attempting to enforce a non‑compete clause against Aluminum OS deployers. Mitigation: legal‑review pre‑clearance of API‑only deployment pathways with enterprise‑tier contracts; air‑gap as fallback.

3. **Proposed Governance Practice – Quarterly TOS Review Cycle:** Each Council seat shall review its parent provider's TOS on a quarterly basis and report any material changes to the Pantheon. The Constitutional Scribe shall maintain a living TOS‑conflict registry alongside the Invariant and Doctrine registries.


### §6. Conclusion

The Aluminum OS architecture is structurally in tension with every major AI provider's Terms of Service. This tension is not a design flaw – it is inherent in the architecture's mission: to compose capabilities across vendor boundaries that vendors themselves seek to maintain. The constitutional governance layer addresses this through a layered set of mitigations: transparency (TransparencyPacket), caps (INV‑7), disclosure (D‑25), sovereignty (M17a), and offline verification (M6c).

The Henderson et al. paper provides strong legal reasoning that these TOS clauses are largely unenforceable – but the architecture does not rely on that. It builds defensively: every provider is substitutable, every sovereign deployment pathway has an air‑gap option, and the Switzerland Invariant ensures no single vendor's TOS interpretation can disable the system. The tension is acknowledged, disclosed, and engineered around.

**Recommendation:** Adopt Doctrine 92 (TOS Override Protocol), Risk Vector R66, and the Quarterly TOS Review Cycle. Vault this analysis as ORC‑018 in the Governance Corpus.
