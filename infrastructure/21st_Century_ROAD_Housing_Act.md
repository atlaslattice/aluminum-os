# 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act: A Comprehensive Reference on Proposed Infrastructure and Construction Work

**Author:** Manus AI
**Date:** March 12, 2026

## Introduction

The **21st Century ROAD to Housing Act** represents a significant bipartisan legislative effort to address the housing affordability and supply crisis in the United States. Passed by the Senate on March 12, 2026, with a vote of 89-10, the bill is a combination of the House-passed *Housing for the 21st Century Act* (H.R. 6644) and the Senate's *Renewing Opportunity in the American Dream (ROAD) to Housing Act* (S. 2651) [1]. Co-sponsored by Senator Tim Scott (R-SC) and Senator Elizabeth Warren (D-MA), the legislation aims to expand the nation's housing supply, modernize key housing and community development programs, and reduce regulatory barriers to construction [2].

This document provides a comprehensive reference to the infrastructure and construction-related provisions within the 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act, drawing from the official section-by-section summary from the Senate Committee on Banking, Housing, and Urban Affairs, as well as analysis from the Bipartisan Policy Center, the National Association of Counties, and legal experts at Latham & Watkins.

## Key Infrastructure and Construction Provisions

The act contains numerous provisions designed to directly and indirectly stimulate housing construction and related infrastructure development. These are primarily concentrated in Title II, "Building More in America," and Title III, "Manufactured Housing for America."

### Title II: Building More in America

This title is the core of the bill's efforts to increase the housing supply through a variety of programs, grants, and regulatory reforms.

## Funding and Financial Incentives

The bill introduces several new funding mechanisms and financial incentives to spur housing development:

*   **Innovation Fund:** A $200 million annual competitive grant program for local governments and tribes that demonstrate measurable increases in housing supply. These funds can be used for a variety of purposes, including improving community infrastructure and supplementing water and sewer grants [2] [4].
*   **Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) Enhancements:** The bill allows up to 20% of CDBG funds to be used for new housing construction. It also introduces the "Build Now Act," which creates a performance-based system, rewarding jurisdictions that exceed median housing growth with bonus CDBG funds, while penalizing those that lag behind [2] [3].
*   **Increased Private Investment:** The "Community Investment and Prosperity Act" provision raises the cap on public welfare investments for banks from 15% to 20%, which is expected to unlock billions of dollars in additional private investment capacity for affordable housing projects [3].
*   **Grants for Planning and Pre-approved Designs:** The bill authorizes grants to help communities with regional housing planning and to establish "pattern books" of pre-approved housing designs to expedite the local construction approval process [3].

## Deregulation and Streamlining

A significant portion of the 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act is dedicated to reducing regulatory burdens that can slow down or increase the cost of housing construction.

*   **NEPA Reform:** The bill enacts significant reforms to the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) review process for housing projects. It creates new categorical exclusions for low-impact projects, such as infill development, rehabilitation of existing structures, minor public infrastructure improvements, and new construction of four or fewer units. This is intended to right-size the environmental review process and get smaller projects to construction more quickly [2] [3].
*   **Streamlined Environmental Reviews:** The "Better Use of Intergovernmental and Local Development (BUILD) Housing Act" provision empowers state, local, and tribal governments to streamline environmental reviews, further reducing delays in housing development [3].
*   **Pre-approved Housing Designs:** The "Accelerating Home Building Act" provision provides grants to help communities adopt pre-approved housing designs, or "pattern books." This will reduce the need for lengthy design review processes for common housing types like accessory dwelling units (ADUs), duplexes, and townhouses [3] [4].

## Conclusion

The 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act is a multifaceted piece of legislation that seeks to address the nation's housing crisis from multiple angles. Its provisions related to infrastructure and construction are extensive, focusing on increasing the housing supply through a combination of direct funding, financial incentives for private investment, and significant regulatory reform. By encouraging the construction of new affordable housing, the rehabilitation of existing stock, the modernization of the manufactured housing industry, and the streamlining of the approval process, the bill aims to make a substantial impact on housing availability and affordability across the United States.

## 1. Introduction

The **21st Century ROAD to Housing Act** is a landmark bipartisan piece of legislation that was passed by the United States Senate on March 12, 2026, with a decisive 89–10 vote. Co-sponsored by Senator Tim Scott (R-SC) and Senator Elizabeth Warren (D-MA), the act represents a significant federal commitment to addressing the nation's housing crisis by directly linking housing development with critical infrastructure investment. The legislation aims to increase the supply of affordable housing, improve housing stability for low-income families, and promote economic development through targeted infrastructure projects.

This document provides a comprehensive reference to the infrastructure build work proposed within the Act, drawing from official summaries and expert analyses to offer a detailed overview of its key provisions and their potential impact on the construction and infrastructure sectors.

## 2. Key Infrastructure Programs

The Act establishes several new grant programs and funding mechanisms to support infrastructure development that is directly tied to housing. These programs are designed to be flexible and responsive to local needs, empowering states and local governments to undertake a wide range of projects.

## 3. Eligible Infrastructure Projects

The Act defines a broad range of infrastructure projects that are eligible for funding under its various grant programs. This flexibility allows communities to address their most pressing needs and to integrate housing development with comprehensive community planning. The following categories represent the primary types of infrastructure work authorized by the legislation:

### 3.1. Transportation Infrastructure

*   **Roads, Bridges, and Sidewalks:** Construction, repair, and improvement of roads, bridges, and sidewalks that provide access to affordable housing developments.
*   **Public Transportation:** Investment in public transportation systems, including the construction of new transit lines, the expansion of existing services, and the purchase of new vehicles.
*   **Bike and Pedestrian Pathways:** Development of dedicated bike lanes and pedestrian pathways to improve connectivity and promote active transportation.

### 3.2. Utilities Infrastructure

*   **Water and Sewer Systems:** Installation and upgrading of water and sewer lines to support new housing construction and to ensure safe and reliable service for existing residents.
*   **Broadband Internet:** Deployment of high-speed broadband internet infrastructure to close the digital divide and to support economic development.
*   **Energy Infrastructure:** Modernization of the electrical grid, including the integration of renewable energy sources and the development of microgrids to improve resilience.

### 3.3. Community Facilities

*   **Schools and Libraries:** Construction and renovation of public schools and libraries to serve the needs of growing communities.
*   **Healthcare Facilities:** Development of community health centers and other healthcare facilities to improve access to care.
*   **Parks and Recreational Facilities:** Creation of new parks, playgrounds, and other recreational facilities to enhance the quality of life for residents.

### 3.4. Resilience and Environmental Infrastructure

*   **Stormwater Management:** Implementation of green infrastructure solutions, such as permeable pavements and rain gardens, to manage stormwater runoff and to reduce flooding.
*   **Coastal Resilience:** Construction of seawalls, levees, and other protective measures to mitigate the impacts of sea-level rise and coastal erosion.
*   **Brownfield Redevelopment:** Remediation and redevelopment of contaminated sites to create new opportunities for housing and economic development.


## 4. Implementation and Timeline

The 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act outlines a phased approach to implementation, with the majority of funding becoming available over a five-year period. The Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD), in coordination with the Department of Transportation (DOT) and the Department of Agriculture (USDA), is responsible for establishing the rules and regulations governing the new grant programs.

The timeline for implementation is as follows:

*   **Year 1:** Federal agencies will develop program guidelines and application processes. A portion of the funding will be made available for planning and capacity-building grants to help communities prepare their applications.
*   **Years 2-4:** The majority of the funding will be disbursed through competitive grant awards. HUD will prioritize projects that demonstrate strong local partnerships, innovative financing mechanisms, and a commitment to long-term affordability.
*   **Year 5:** The final round of funding will be awarded, and federal agencies will conduct a comprehensive evaluation of the Act's impact on housing supply, affordability, and economic development.
